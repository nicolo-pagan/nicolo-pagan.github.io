About Me

I am a computational data science engineer with a multi-disciplinary background in the social, behavioural, and network sciences.

Since May 2021, I am a Post-doctoral Researcher at the Social Computing Group at the University of Zürich. In April 2021, I completed my doctoral degree at the Automatic Control Lab at ETH Zürich under the supervision of Prof. Florian Dörfler. My primary research interests lie in the area of social media platform dynamics , where I explore the complex interplay between AI and human decision-making. My final goal is to design effective interventions that foster social well-being.

I consider myself half-mathematician and half-computer scientist, with strong analytical skills and creative thinking. Broadly speaking, I like mathematical modelling of social, natural, and engineering systems.

During my spare time, I enjoy playing chess, practicing any kind of outdoor activity (especially on the mountains), travelling, photographing. I love beautiful natural landscapes and architecture.

Research

My primary research interests lie in the area of social media platform dynamics, where I explore the complex interplay between technology and human behavior in an intricate network system. Through investigating these dynamics, I aim to gain insights into the effects (and especially the drawbacks) of social media on individuals, groups, and society as a whole. Through my expertise in mathematical modeling, complex systems analysis, and data science, I am able to delve into this field of study with a unique perspective.

The significance of this research is driven by three primary factors.

  • Firstly, social media platforms have a rapidly growing impact on shaping human cognition and behavior in essentially all domains, including information, education, labor market, and healthcare. At the same time, online platforms offer ample opportunities for researchers to quantitatively study these complex processes.
  • Secondly, AI-powered recommendation engines pose critical challenges in regard to the quality, fairness, and type of information that users are exposed to.
  • Finally, this research is inherently interdisciplinary, offering opportunities at the crossroads of communication, social, economic, and engineering sciences.

More broadly speaking, I am interested in Social and Engineering Systems and in problems that correspond to significant societal challenges, with an emphasis on areas such as autonomous systems, energy systems, finance, social networks, urban systems, and their underlying interconnections. In particular, I try to put an emphasis on the fairness aspects.

Dynamics of Social Networks

The field that studies the dynamics of social networks aims to understand how social connections between individuals change over time. My primary research focus in this area is to build mathematical models that are both sociologically grounded and computationally feasible. These models consider the strategic incentives that drive individuals to form connections with others, such as shared interests or access to valuable resources. By using a game-theoretical approach, I developed a method for learning these individual preferences from social network data [J1]. My research provided sociological and strategic interpretations of random network models and yielded results that align with empirical, historical, and sociological observations. In more recent work, I focused on the network formation process surrounding the rise of social media influencers on popular platforms [J2]. Inspired by Twitter data analysis, I proposed a meritocratic network formation model where followers continuously search for the best user-generated content provider. The model’s predictions, validated against data collected from Twitch, a popular platform for gaming and video streaming, highlighted the importance of content quality in network formation.

Dynamics on Social Networks

In addition to examining the structural evolution of social networks over time, it is crucial to analyze the internal dynamics on these networks. This involves investigating how information spreads within networks and how individual behaviors and attitudes are shaped by their social connections. To this end, I conducted both theoretical and empirical analyses. Regarding my theoretical contribution, I delved into the closed (feedback) loop between personalized recommender systems and opinion dynamics and I discovered that recommender systems possess the ability to steer individual opinions at the microscopic level, even if the overall distribution of opinions remains unchanged at the macroscopic level [C5]. This finding raises important questions about the potential for significant distortion of individual opinions, even in situations where the overall macroscopic situation appears unaltered. Additionally, I provided a classification framework for the different types of feedback loops that occur in recommender systems, and in machine-learning-based decision-making systems more broadly [C7]. I demonstrated through simulations that feedback loops can perpetuate, reinforce, or even reduce biases, providing important insights into the impact of these systems in various domains. With respect to empirical work, I collected and analyzed Twitter data to study public debates surrounding the Covid-19 vaccination in Italy and abortion rights in the US. The aim was to integrate the analysis of users’ opinions and emotional states, as well as toxic behavior, by using natural language processing techniques and network analysis. Overall, this research provided valuable insights into the complex dynamics on social networks and their potential influence on individual opinions and behaviors.

Interventions on Social Media Platforms

As social media platforms continue to grow in popularity, they are not only becoming a hub for connecting with others but are also increasingly being leveraged as a means of making a living. The Creator Economy has seen tremendous growth over the past decade, with more than 50 million content creators and a market worth over $104 billion. However, social media platforms do not always provide fair opportunities and compensation for these creators. My research in this area focuses on how various recommender systems impact fairness for content creators. Building on the meritocratic network formation model [J2] and through extensive computational analysis, I found that increasing the exploration of the recommender system generally leads to fairer outcomes for creators [C3]. However, and perhaps surprisingly, I also found that beyond a certain threshold, exploration ultimately diminishes fairness for creators [J3]. In my latest work, I further investigated the impact of potentially biased recommender systems in the presence of users’ homophilic behavior [C6]. The results of the study indicated that interventions designed to support protected users, such as those from minority groups, can be effective, but require additional care.

Teaching

Early in my doctoral studies, I acquired teaching experience through various opportunities, including serving as a substitute instructor and head teaching assistant for undergraduate and graduate courses, as well as by delivering tutorial presentations. In spring 2021, I lectured in the course Social Computing, which focuses on the intersection between social behavior and computing systems. The aim of the course is to educate students on the impact of online platforms on human perception, social interaction, and the formation of societal norms. Through the course, I taught students how to formulate relevant research questions in this domain, how to gather data from various social media platforms, and how to analyze this data to answer their research questions.
